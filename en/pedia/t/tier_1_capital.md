# Tier 1 Capital

Tier 1 [capital](../c/capital.md), often referred to as "core [capital](../c/capital.md)," is a critical measure of a [bank](../b/bank.md)'s financial strength. It is a primary [indicator](../i/indicator.md) used by banking regulators to assess the stability and [solvency](../s/solvency.md) of financial institutions. In this detailed discussion, we [will](../w/will.md) explore the components, importance, and regulatory aspects of Tier 1 [capital](../c/capital.md), along with its role in [bank](../b/bank.md) [risk management](../r/risk_management.md) and its impact on the global [financial system](../f/financial_system.md).

## Components of Tier 1 Capital

Tier 1 [capital](../c/capital.md) primarily consists of the following elements:

### 1. Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1)

CET1 includes:

- **Common [shares](../s/shares.md)**: These are the regular [shares](../s/shares.md) traded on stock exchanges.
- **[Retained earnings](../r/retained_earnings.md)**: These are the profits that a [bank](../b/bank.md) decides to keep rather than distribute as dividends.
- **[Accumulated other comprehensive income](../a/accumulated_other_comprehensive_income.md) (AOCI)**: This includes [unrealized gains](../u/unrealized_gains.md) and losses on certain types of financial instruments and investments.
- **Regulatory adjustments**: These involve deductions for items such as [goodwill](../g/goodwill.md), deferred tax assets, and intangible assets that can reduce CET1 [capital](../c/capital.md).

### 2. Additional Tier 1 (AT1)

AT1 includes:

- **Preferred [shares](../s/shares.md)**: These have characteristics of both [equity](../e/equity.md) and [debt](../d/debt.md) but are considered less secure than common [shares](../s/shares.md).
- **[Contingent convertible](../c/contingent_convertible.md) securities (CoCos)**: These can be converted into [equity](../e/equity.md) or written down in certain conditions, such as when the [bank](../b/bank.md)'s [capital](../c/capital.md) falls below a specific threshold.

## Importance of Tier 1 Capital

The significance of Tier 1 [capital](../c/capital.md) can be understood by examining its role in the following aspects:

### 1. Solvency and Stability

Tier 1 [capital](../c/capital.md) indicates a [bank](../b/bank.md)'s ability to sustain losses without triggering [bankruptcy](../b/bankruptcy.md). A high [Tier 1 capital ratio](../t/tier_1_capital_ratio.md) implies that a [bank](../b/bank.md) has a substantial cushion to absorb unexpected losses, thereby promoting the institution's long-term stability.

### 2. Regulatory Compliance

Tier 1 [capital](../c/capital.md) is central to regulatory frameworks like [Basel III](../b/basel_iii.md), which require banks to maintain minimum [capital](../c/capital.md) ratios. These regulations are designed to enhance the resilience of the banking sector by ensuring that banks [hold](../h/hold.md) sufficient high-quality [capital](../c/capital.md).

### 3. Investor Confidence

A strong Tier 1 [capital](../c/capital.md) position boosts [investor](../i/investor.md) confidence. It signals that a [bank](../b/bank.md) is well-capitalized and can withstand financial shocks, making it an attractive investment.

### 4. Credit Rating

[Credit rating](../c/credit_rating.md) agencies consider Tier 1 [capital](../c/capital.md) ratios when evaluating the [creditworthiness](../c/creditworthiness.md) of banks. Higher ratios generally lead to better [credit](../c/credit.md) ratings, reducing the cost of borrowing and expanding funding opportunities.

## Regulatory Frameworks: Basel Accords

The [Basel Accords](../b/basel_accords.md), formulated by the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS), provide a comprehensive framework for banking regulations on [capital](../c/capital.md) adequacy. The key regulations under the [Basel Accords](../b/basel_accords.md) include:

### 1. Basel I

Introduced in 1988, [Basel I](../b/basel_i.md) focused on [credit risk](../c/credit_risk.md) and established a minimum [capital](../c/capital.md) requirement of 8% of [risk-weighted assets](../r/risk-weighted_assets.md).

### 2. Basel II

Released in 2004, [Basel II](../b/basel_ii.md) refined the framework by incorporating a more sophisticated approach to [risk management](../r/risk_management.md), including operational and [market](../m/market.md) risks, alongside [credit risk](../c/credit_risk.md).

### 3. Basel III

Introduced in response to the 2007-2008 [financial crisis](../f/financial_crisis.md), [Basel III](../b/basel_iii.md) significantly enhanced the [capital](../c/capital.md) requirements:

- **Minimum Common [Equity](../e/equity.md) Tier 1 (CET1) ratio**: 4.5%
- **Minimum [Tier 1 capital ratio](../t/tier_1_capital_ratio.md)**: 6%
- **Minimum Total [Capital](../c/capital.md) Ratio**: 8%
- **Conservation buffer**: An additional 2.5% of CET1 [capital](../c/capital.md), bringing the total CET1 requirement to 7%.
- **Countercyclical buffer**: Ranges from 0% to 2.5% of CET1 [capital](../c/capital.md) based on national circumstances.

[Basel III](../b/basel_iii.md) also introduced stringent [liquidity](../l/liquidity.md) requirements through the [Liquidity](../l/liquidity.md) [Coverage Ratio](../c/coverage_ratio.md) (LCR) and Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR) to ensure banks maintain adequate [liquidity](../l/liquidity.md) levels.

## Risk Management

Tier 1 [capital](../c/capital.md) is a cornerstone of [risk management](../r/risk_management.md) in the banking sector. Banks must maintain sufficient Tier 1 [capital](../c/capital.md) to cover different types of [risk](../r/risk.md):

### 1. Credit Risk

[Credit risk](../c/credit_risk.md) arises from the potential that borrowers [will](../w/will.md) [default](../d/default.md) on their [obligations](../o/obligation.md). Banks use Tier 1 [capital](../c/capital.md) to absorb losses resulting from [loan](../l/loan.md) defaults, ensuring they remain solvent.

### 2. Market Risk

This [risk](../r/risk.md) stems from adverse movements in [market](../m/market.md) prices, such as [interest](../i/interest.md) rates, [foreign exchange](../f/foreign_exchange.md) rates, and [equity](../e/equity.md) prices. Tier 1 [capital](../c/capital.md) provides a buffer against potential losses from [market](../m/market.md) [volatility](../v/volatility.md).

### 3. Operational Risk

[Operational risk](../o/operational_risk.md) includes losses due to internal failures, such as [fraud](../f/fraud.md), system failures, or other disruptions. Adequate Tier 1 [capital](../c/capital.md) ensures that a [bank](../b/bank.md) can continue operations despite such incidents.

## Impact on the Global Financial System

Tier 1 [capital](../c/capital.md) plays a vital role in maintaining the global [financial system](../f/financial_system.md)'s stability:

### 1. Financial Crisis Mitigation

During financial crises, well-capitalized banks can absorb significant losses without collapsing, preventing a domino effect that could destabilize the entire [financial system](../f/financial_system.md).

### 2. Economic Stability

Banks with [robust](../r/robust.md) Tier 1 [capital](../c/capital.md) are better equipped to provide [credit](../c/credit.md) in times of economic stress, supporting businesses and households. This helps maintain economic stability and promotes recovery during downturns.

### 3. Cross-Border Operations

Global banks operate across [multiple](../m/multiple.md) jurisdictions. The standardized requirements for Tier 1 [capital](../c/capital.md) under [Basel III](../b/basel_iii.md) ensure a level playing field, facilitating cross-border banking operations and enhancing international financial stability.

## Future Trends and Developments

The landscape of Tier 1 [capital](../c/capital.md) is continually evolving, influenced by various factors:

### 1. Technological Advancements

Advances in financial technology (fintech) are reshaping the banking [industry](../i/industry.md). Fintech innovations, such as [blockchain](../b/blockchain_in_trading.md) and [artificial intelligence](../a/artificial_intelligence_in_trading.md), [offer](../o/offer.md) new ways to enhance [risk management](../r/risk_management.md) and [capital allocation](../c/capital_allocation.md).

### 2. Regulatory Changes

Regulatory bodies periodically review and update [capital](../c/capital.md) requirements to reflect changing [economic conditions](../e/economic_conditions.md) and emerging risks. Banks must stay attuned to these changes to ensure continued compliance.

### 3. Climate Risk

As awareness of climate change grows, banks are increasingly considering climate-related financial risks. This may lead to new regulatory requirements and adjustments in [capital](../c/capital.md) planning.
