# Weighted Average Coupon (WAC)

The [Weighted Average](../w/weighted_average.md) Coupon (WAC) is a financial metric used primarily in the [mortgage](../m/mortgage.md)-backed securities (MBS) [industry](../i/industry.md). It represents the average [interest rate](../i/interest_rate.md) of a pool of loans, mortgages, or other assets, [weighted](../w/weighted.md) by the balance of each [loan](../l/loan.md) at a given point in time. Understanding the WAC is essential for investors and analysts to assess the [yield](../y/yield.md), [risk](../r/risk.md), and performance characteristics of a specific MBS. 

To elucidate the concept more thoroughly, we'll explore various facets of WAC, including its calculation, significance in the financial [industry](../i/industry.md), relationship with other securities, and real-world applications in [portfolio management](../p/par.md).

## Calculation of WAC

The WAC is calculated by summing the products of the coupon rates and their corresponding weights (which are typically the [principal](../p/principal.md) balances of the individual loans) and then dividing the result by the total [principal](../p/principal.md) balance. The formula for WAC is represented as follows:

```markdown
\[ \text{WAC} = \frac{\sum (R_i \times P_i)}{\sum P_i} \]

where,
R_i = [Coupon rate](../c/coupon_rate.md) of the individual [loan](../l/loan.md)
P_i = [Principal](../p/principal.md) balance of the individual [loan](../l/loan.md)
```

To put this formula into perspective, let's consider a simple example:

1. [Loan](../l/loan.md) 1: $100,000 at 4.5% [coupon rate](../c/coupon_rate.md) (R1)
2. [Loan](../l/loan.md) 2: $150,000 at 5.0% [coupon rate](../c/coupon_rate.md) (R2)
3. [Loan](../l/loan.md) 3: $250,000 at 4.8% [coupon rate](../c/coupon_rate.md) (R3)

The WAC would be calculated as follows:

```markdown
\[ \text{WAC} = \frac{(100,000 \times 0.045) + (150,000 \times 0.05) + (250,000 \times 0.048)}{100,000 + 150,000 + 250,000} \]

\[ \text{WAC} = \frac{4,500 + 7,500 + 12,000}{500,000} \]

\[ \text{WAC} = \frac{24,000}{500,000} \]
\[ \text{WAC} = 0.048 \text{ or } 4.8\% \]
```

In this example, the WAC of the pool of loans would be 4.8%.

## Importance of WAC in Mortgage-Backed Securities (MBS)

The concept of WAC is particularly significant in the context of MBS for several reasons:

### Yield Analysis

The WAC offers investors a way to determine the potential [yield](../y/yield.md) of a [mortgage](../m/mortgage.md)-backed [security](../s/security.md). Higher WAC values generally indicate higher [interest](../i/interest.md) payments, which can lead to higher returns for investors. Conversely, lower WAC values may suggest lower yields.

### Risk Assessment

WAC can serve as an [indicator](../i/indicator.md) of the [credit](../c/credit.md) and [interest rate risk](../i/interest_rate_risk.md) associated with a pool of mortgages. For instance, a higher WAC might suggest that the loans in the pool carry higher [interest](../i/interest.md) rates due to borrowers' higher [risk profiles](../r/risk_profiles.md). Understanding this aspect helps investors make more informed decisions regarding the [risk](../r/risk.md) they are assuming.

### Prepayment Dynamics

Prepayments can significantly affect the WAC over time. Prepayments occur when borrowers pay off their mortgages early, which typically happens when [interest](../i/interest.md) rates decline, prompting refinancing. As high-[interest](../i/interest.md) loans are paid off faster, the WAC of the remaining loans may decrease. Monitoring the WAC helps in predicting and managing the impact of [prepayment](../p/prepayment.md) on the overall [return](../r/return.md) of the MBS.

## Relationship with Other Metrics

### Weighted Average Maturity (WAM)

WAM is another critical metric that often goes hand-in-hand with WAC. While WAC focuses on the average [interest rate](../i/interest_rate.md), WAM looks at the average time until the loans in the pool mature, [weighted](../w/weighted.md) by their [principal](../p/principal.md) balance. Both metrics together can provide a more comprehensive picture of the MBS in terms of its expected returns and risks.

### Weighted Average Loan Age (WALA)

WALA measures the average age of the loans in the pool, [weighted](../w/weighted.md) by their balances. This gives insight into how seasoned the loans are, which can further impact the [prepayment](../p/prepayment.md) rates and, consequently, the WAC.

### Spread Analysis

Analysts compare the WAC with other [interest rate](../i/interest_rate.md) benchmarks, such as the [yield](../y/yield.md) on [U.S. Treasury](../u/u.s._treasury.md) securities or LIBOR. This [spread analysis](../s/spread_analysis.md) helps in understanding the relative [risk premium](../r/risk_premium.md) associated with the MBS.

## Applications in Portfolio Management

WAC plays a critical role in [portfolio management](../p/par.md), especially for institutions that invest heavily in [mortgage](../m/mortgage.md)-backed securities. Here are some specific applications:

### Diversification Strategy

Portfolio managers use WAC to diversify their [holdings](../h/holdings.md) across different types of [mortgage](../m/mortgage.md)-backed securities. By balancing securities with different WACs, they can achieve a more consistent [return](../r/return.md) while managing [risk](../r/risk.md).

### Performance Monitoring

Regular monitoring of the WAC helps in assessing the performance of the [mortgage](../m/mortgage.md) portfolio. Changes in the WAC over time can indicate shifts in the [interest rate](../i/interest_rate.md) environment or borrower behavior, prompting necessary adjustments to the portfolio.

### Hedging Strategies

Understanding the WAC is vital for developing effective [hedging strategies](../h/hedging_strategies.md). For instance, if the WAC is expected to decline due to increasing prepayments, managers might [hedge](../h/hedge.md) against [interest rate](../i/interest_rate.md) changes to protect the portfolio's [value](../v/value.md).

## Real-World Examples

### Agency Mortgage-Backed Securities

Agency MBS are issued by government-sponsored enterprises (GSEs) like Fannie Mae, [Freddie Mac](../f/freddie_mac.md), or Ginnie Mae. The WAC for these securities is generally lower due to the high [credit](../c/credit.md) quality of the [underlying](../u/underlying.md) mortgages. Investors rely on the WAC to gauge the [expected return](../e/expected_return.md) and [risk](../r/risk.md) of these relatively low-[risk](../r/risk.md) securities.

### Non-Agency Mortgage-Backed Securities

Non-agency MBS do not have the backing of GSEs and therefore carry higher [credit risk](../c/credit_risk.md). The WAC of non-agency MBS is typically higher to compensate for this increased [risk](../r/risk.md). Investors need to scrutinize the WAC alongside other [credit](../c/credit.md) metrics to make well-informed investment decisions.
