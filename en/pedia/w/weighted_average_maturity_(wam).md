# Weighted Average Maturity (WAM)

[Weighted Average](../w/weighted_average.md) [Maturity](../m/maturity.md) (WAM) is a critical metric in the field of [finance](../f/finance.md), particularly for [bond](../b/bond.md) [investing](../i/investing.md), [portfolio management](../p/par.md), [money market](../m/money_market.md) funds, and [mortgage](../m/mortgage.md)-backed securities. It offers investors, [fund](../f/fund.md) managers, and financial analysts an insight into the average time until the securities within a portfolio or a [fund](../f/fund.md) mature, taking into account the weight of each [security](../s/security.md)'s contribution. This measure aids in the assessment of [interest rate risk](../i/interest_rate_risk.md), [liquidity](../l/liquidity.md), and overall [risk](../r/risk.md) exposure of a portfolio.

## Definition and Calculation

[Weighted Average](../w/weighted_average.md) [Maturity](../m/maturity.md) is defined as the average time until all the securities in a portfolio mature, [weighted](../w/weighted.md) by the [value](../v/value.md) of each [security](../s/security.md). The formula for calculating WAM is straightforward and can be expressed as:

\[ \text{WAM} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left( \frac{V_i}{V} \times M_i \right) \]

Where:
- \( n \) is the total number of securities in the portfolio.
- \( V_i \) is the dollar [value](../v/value.md) of the \( i \)-th [security](../s/security.md).
- \( V \) is the total dollar [value](../v/value.md) of the portfolio.
- \( M_i \) is the [maturity](../m/maturity.md) of the \( i \)-th [security](../s/security.md) in years.

By using this formula, investors can determine the time-[weighted](../w/weighted.md) [maturity](../m/maturity.md) of their portfolio, which accounts for each [security](../s/security.md)'s proportionate [value](../v/value.md) within the portfolio.

## Importance in Different Financial Products

### Bonds and Fixed-Income Portfolios

In [bond](../b/bond.md) [investing](../i/investing.md), WAM is used to understand the average time to the portfolio’s cash flows. Investors use this metric to gauge [interest rate risk](../i/interest_rate_risk.md), since bonds with longer maturities are generally more sensitive to [interest rate](../i/interest_rate.md) changes compared to short-term bonds.

### Money Market Funds

[Money market](../m/money_market.md) funds, which invest in short-term, high-quality securities, use WAM to maintain [liquidity](../l/liquidity.md) and stability. Regulatory bodies, such as the U.S. Securities and [Exchange](../e/exchange.md) [Commission](../c/commission.md) (SEC), have guidelines that require [money market](../m/money_market.md) funds to keep their WAM below a certain threshold. For example, according to the SEC's Rule 2a-7, the WAM should be no more than 60 days for a [money market fund](../m/money_market_fund.md).

### Mortgage-Backed Securities

[Mortgage](../m/mortgage.md)-backed securities (MBS) also utilize WAM to measure the average time until the mortgages [underlying](../u/underlying.md) the securities are paid off. This metric helps in predicting cash flows and assessing [risk](../r/risk.md) since MBS are prone to [prepayment risk](../p/prepayment_risk.md), which can alter the [maturity](../m/maturity.md) of the securities.

## Implications of WAM

### Interest Rate Risk

WAM provides an insight into the sensitivity of a portfolio to changes in [interest](../i/interest.md) rates. A higher WAM indicates that the portfolio is more sensitive to [interest rate](../i/interest_rate.md) fluctuations, which could lead to higher [volatility](../v/volatility.md) in terms of price changes. Conversely, a lower WAM suggests a lower sensitivity to [interest rate](../i/interest_rate.md) changes.

### Liquidity

The WAM of a portfolio can give investors an idea of its [liquidity](../l/liquidity.md) profile. Shorter WAM generally indicates higher [liquidity](../l/liquidity.md) because the securities mature sooner, providing quicker access to cash. This is particularly important for [money market](../m/money_market.md) funds and other instruments where [liquidity](../l/liquidity.md) is a key concern.

### Yield

Typically, longer WAM is associated with higher yields because investors [demand](../d/demand.md) a [premium](../p/premium.md) for taking on the additional [interest rate risk](../i/interest_rate_risk.md) and reduced [liquidity](../l/liquidity.md). This relationship is a fundamental concept in the [term structure of interest rates](../t/term_structure_of_interest_rates.md).

## Example Calculation

Consider a simplified portfolio composed of the following securities:

1. [Security](../s/security.md) A: [Value](../v/value.md) = $1,000,000; [Maturity](../m/maturity.md) = 2 years
2. [Security](../s/security.md) B: [Value](../v/value.md) = $500,000; [Maturity](../m/maturity.md) = 1 year
3. [Security](../s/security.md) C: [Value](../v/value.md) = $1,500,000; [Maturity](../m/maturity.md) = 3 years

The total [value](../v/value.md) of the portfolio (\( V \)) is $3,000,000.

Using the WAM formula:

\[ \text{WAM} = \left( \frac{1,000,000}{3,000,000} \times 2 \right) + \left( \frac{500,000}{3,000,000} \times 1 \right) + \left( \frac{1,500,000}{3,000,000} \times 3 \right) \]

\[ \text{WAM} = \left( 0.333 \times 2 \right) + \left( 0.167 \times 1 \right) + \left( 0.5 \times 3 \right) \]

\[ \text{WAM} = 0.666 + 0.167 + 1.5 \]

\[ \text{WAM} = 2.333 \text{ years} \]

Therefore, the [Weighted Average](../w/weighted_average.md) [Maturity](../m/maturity.md) of this portfolio is approximately 2.33 years.

## Managing Weighted Average Maturity

### Strategies

To manage WAM, portfolio managers might employ various strategies such as:

1. **[Laddering](../l/laddering.md):** This involves creating a portfolio of bonds with varying maturities. As shorter-term bonds mature, the proceeds are reinvested into longer-term bonds to maintain a balanced [maturity](../m/maturity.md) profile.
2. **Immunization:** This strategy structures a portfolio in such a way that its [duration](../d/duration.md) matches the [investment horizon](../i/investment_horizon.md), minimizing [interest rate risk](../i/interest_rate_risk.md).
3. **[Active Management](../a/active_management.md):** Regularly adjusting the composition of the portfolio to respond to changing [market](../m/market.md) conditions, [interest](../i/interest.md) rates, and economic forecasts.

## Regulatory Considerations

Regulatory bodies often set guidelines around WAM to ensure [liquidity](../l/liquidity.md) and reduce [risk](../r/risk.md) in certain types of funds. For example, the SEC’s Rule 2a-7, which governs [money market](../m/money_market.md) funds, stipulates that these funds must maintain a WAM of no more than 60 days. This regulation ensures that [money market](../m/money_market.md) funds are able to meet [redemption](../r/redemption.md) requests without significant [liquidity risk](../l/liquidity_risk.md).

In the context of [mortgage](../m/mortgage.md)-backed securities, WAM is essential in stress-testing portfolios to ensure they can withstand varying [interest rate](../i/interest_rate.md) environments and [prepayment](../p/prepayment.md) scenarios.
