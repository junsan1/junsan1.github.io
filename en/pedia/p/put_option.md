# Put Option

A put option is a financial contract that gives the owner the right, but not the obligation, to sell a specified amount of an [underlying asset](../u/underlying_asset.md) at a predetermined price (known as the [strike price](../s/strike_price.md)) within a specified time period. This instrument is commonly used in stock markets to [hedge](../h/hedge.md) against potential losses or to speculate on the decline of a particular [asset](../a/asset.md). Let's explore the various aspects and intricacies of [put options](../p/put_options.md).

## Understanding Put Options

### Basic Mechanics

A put option is essentially a bet that the price of the [underlying asset](../u/underlying_asset.md) [will](../w/will.md) fall below the [strike price](../s/strike_price.md) before the option expires. The buyer of the put option pays a [premium](../p/premium.md) for purchasing this right. If the price of the [underlying asset](../u/underlying_asset.md) does decline beneath the [strike price](../s/strike_price.md), the holder of the put option can [exercise](../e/exercise.md) the option to sell the [asset](../a/asset.md) at the higher [strike price](../s/strike_price.md), thus making a [profit](../p/profit.md). Conversely, if the [asset](../a/asset.md) price stays above the [strike price](../s/strike_price.md), the put option may expire worthless, limiting the loss to the [premium](../p/premium.md) paid.

### Key Terms

1. **[Premium](../p/premium.md)**: The cost paid by the buyer to the seller ([writer](../w/writer.md)) of the put option.
2. **[Strike Price](../s/strike_price.md)**: The price at which the put option holder can sell the [underlying asset](../u/underlying_asset.md).
3. **[Expiration Date](../e/expiration_date.md)**: The date on which the option contract becomes void.
4. **[Underlying Asset](../u/underlying_asset.md)**: The [financial instrument](../f/financial_instrument.md) on which the put option is based, such as [stocks](../s/stock.md), bonds, or commodities.

### Example

Suppose you own a stock currently trading at $100. You buy a put option with a [strike price](../s/strike_price.md) of $95, expiring in one month, for a [premium](../p/premium.md) of $5. If the stock's price falls to $90, you can [exercise](../e/exercise.md) the option to sell the stock at $95, [netting](../n/netting.md) a [profit](../p/profit.md) of $5 per share (ignoring the [premium](../p/premium.md)). If the stock remains at $100 or rises, the put option expires worthless, and you lose the $5 [premium](../p/premium.md).

## Types of Put Options

### American vs. European

- **American-style**: These [options](../o/options.md) can be exercised at any time up to the [expiration date](../e/expiration_date.md).
- **European-style**: These [options](../o/options.md) can only be exercised at the [expiration date](../e/expiration_date.md).

### Cash-settled vs. Physically-settled

- **Cash-settled**: The option is settled in cash rather than the delivery of the [asset](../a/asset.md).
- **Physically-settled**: The [underlying asset](../u/underlying_asset.md) is delivered upon [exercise](../e/exercise.md) of the put option.

## Applications

### Hedging

[Put options](../p/put_options.md) are widely used as a hedging tool. For example, an [investor](../i/investor.md) holding a portfolio of [stocks](../s/stock.md) may buy [put options](../p/put_options.md) to protect against a potential [market](../m/market.md) downturn. This strategy is akin to buying [insurance](../i/insurance.md) — the [put options](../p/put_options.md) "insure" the portfolio against significant losses.

### Speculation

Traders often use [put options](../p/put_options.md) to speculate on the decline of an [asset](../a/asset.md). By purchasing [put options](../p/put_options.md), they can [leverage](../l/leverage.md) a relatively small amount of [capital](../c/capital.md) into a potentially large [profit](../p/profit.md) if their prediction is correct.

### Arbitrage

Arbitrageurs can exploit price discrepancies between the put option, the [underlying asset](../u/underlying_asset.md), and other financial instruments. Strategies such as [put-call parity](../p/put-call_parity.md) involve using [put options](../p/put_options.md), call [options](../o/options.md), and the [underlying asset](../u/underlying_asset.md) to lock in [risk](../r/risk.md)-free profits.

## Pricing Put Options

### Black-Scholes Model

One of the most renowned models for pricing [options](../o/options.md) is the [Black-Scholes model](../b/black-scholes_model.md). It takes into account factors like the current price of the [underlying asset](../u/underlying_asset.md), the [strike price](../s/strike_price.md), the time to expiration, [risk](../r/risk.md)-free [interest rate](../i/interest_rate.md), and the [asset](../a/asset.md)'s [volatility](../v/volatility.md).

### Binomial Model

The binomial model considers [multiple](../m/multiple.md) periods and can [handle](../h/handle.md) American-style [options](../o/options.md) more effectively than the [Black-Scholes model](../b/black-scholes_model.md). It works by creating a binomial tree of possible [asset](../a/asset.md) prices and evaluates the option at each node.

### Factors Affecting Put Option Prices

- **[Intrinsic Value](../i/intrinsic_value.md)**: The difference between the [strike price](../s/strike_price.md) and the [underlying asset](../u/underlying_asset.md)’s current price, if the option is in-the-[money](../m/money.md).
- **[Time Value](../t/time_value.md)**: The additional [value](../v/value.md) attributed to the remaining time until expiration.
- **[Volatility](../v/volatility.md)**: Higher [volatility](../v/volatility.md) increases the price of the put option as the likelihood of the [asset](../a/asset.md) price falling below the [strike price](../s/strike_price.md) increases.
- **[Interest Rate](../i/interest_rate.md)**: Higher [risk](../r/risk.md)-free [interest](../i/interest.md) rates tend to decrease the price of [put options](../p/put_options.md).

## Strategies Involving Put Options

### Protective Put

In this strategy, an [investor](../i/investor.md) buys a put option for an [asset](../a/asset.md) they already own. This protects against [downside risk](../d/downside_risk.md) while allowing for [upside potential](../u/upside_potential_in_trading.md). If the [asset](../a/asset.md)’s price falls, losses are [offset](../o/offset.md) by gains from the put option.

### Long Put

The [long put](../l/long_put.md) strategy involves simply buying a put option with the expectation that the [underlying asset](../u/underlying_asset.md)’s price [will](../w/will.md) decline. This strategy offers high potential returns but carries the [risk](../r/risk.md) of losing the [premium](../p/premium.md) paid.

### Bear Put Spread

Involves buying a put option at a higher [strike price](../s/strike_price.md) and selling another put option at a lower [strike price](../s/strike_price.md). This reduces the [premium](../p/premium.md) paid while capping potential profits.

### Synthetic Short Stock

This strategy involves combining a [long put](../l/long_put.md) with a [short call](../s/short_call.md) (at the same [strike price](../s/strike_price.md) and expiration) to simulate a [short sale](../s/short_sale.md) of the [underlying asset](../u/underlying_asset.md). This can be used to [hedge](../h/hedge.md) or speculate on price declines without actually selling short.

## Risks and Considerations

### Limited Time

[Put options](../p/put_options.md) have an [expiration date](../e/expiration_date.md), which means the investment can become worthless if not adequately timed. The [time decay](../t/time_decay.md), or "[theta](../t/theta.md)," is a crucial [factor](../f/factor.md), and [options](../o/options.md) lose [value](../v/value.md) as expiration approaches.

### Market Risks

While [put options](../p/put_options.md) mitigate against certain [market](../m/market.md) risks, they do not eliminate all of them. Factors such as [liquidity](../l/liquidity.md), [market](../m/market.md) mechanics, and broad [economic conditions](../e/economic_conditions.md) can affect the performance of a put option.

### Premiums

Paying the [premium](../p/premium.md) for a put option constitutes an upfront cost. In scenarios where the put option expires worthless, the entire [premium](../p/premium.md) is lost. Hence, it's essential to carefully weigh the cost against the hedging or speculative benefits.

### Counterparty Risk

In some cases, especially with Over-The-Counter (OTC) [options](../o/options.md), there's a [risk](../r/risk.md) that the [counterparty](../c/counterparty.md) may [default](../d/default.md) on their [obligations](../o/obligation.md).

## Real-World Examples

### Portfolio Management

A [hedge fund](../h/hedge_fund.md) that has a significant exposure to tech [stocks](../s/stock.md) might purchase [put options](../p/put_options.md) on a tech-heavy [index](../i/index.md) like the [NASDAQ](../n/nasdaq.md). This way, if the tech sector experiences a downturn, the losses in the portfolio would be [offset](../o/offset.md) by gains from the [put options](../p/put_options.md).

### Corporate Finance

Companies sometimes use [put options](../p/put_options.md) as part of their financial strategy. For example, a company that has issued convertible bonds may buy [put options](../p/put_options.md) to [hedge](../h/hedge.md) against the [risk](../r/risk.md) that the stock price falls and the bonds get converted into [equity](../e/equity.md) at an unfavorable rate.

## Conclusion

[Put options](../p/put_options.md) are versatile financial tools that [offer](../o/offer.md) the ability to [hedge](../h/hedge.md) against risks, speculate on declines, and ensure strategic [corporate finance](../c/corporate_finance.md) practices. While they provide potential for significant gains, they also come with inherent risks, primarily the loss of premiums and potential expiration worthlessness.
