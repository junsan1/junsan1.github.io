# Voluntary Export Restraint (VER)

A Voluntary [Export](../e/export.md) Restraint (VER) is a self-imposed limitation by an exporting country on the quantity of goods it ships to another country. Unlike other types of [trade](../t/trade.md) barriers, a VER is not a formal policy dictated by the importing country's government. Instead, it is an agreement reached between the exporting and importing countries where the former agrees to restrict its exports to prevent stricter measures like tariffs or [quotas](../q/quota.md) from being imposed by the importing country.

## Historical Context and Examples

VERs have been used primarily to protect industries in the importing country from competition. One of the most notable examples of a VER occurred in the 1980s when the United States faced a surge in imported Japanese automobiles. To stave off tariffs and other [trade sanctions](../t/trade_sanction.md), Japan agreed to limit the number of cars it exported to the U.S. This VER allowed the U.S. auto [industry](../i/industry.md) to recover and adjust to the competition without experiencing the sudden shock of unrestricted imports.

Another example is the agreement between the [European Union](../e/european_union_(eu).md) and China on textile exports in 2005. The EU was concerned about the flood of cheap textiles from China after the end of the Multi-Fiber Arrangement (MFA). To avert restrictive measures from the EU, China agreed to a VER to limit its textile exports.

These examples illustrate how VERs are used as a diplomatic tool to balance international [trade](../t/trade.md) relations and protect domestic industries from foreign competition without resorting to more severe [trade](../t/trade.md) barriers.

## Purpose and Mechanisms

The primary objectives of a VER include:

- **Protecting Domestic Industries:** VERs are often employed to give domestic industries time to restructure and become more competitive without the heavy competition from an influx of foreign goods. For example, in the case of the U.S. auto [industry](../i/industry.md), the VER with Japan provided breathing space for American car manufacturers to enhance their competitiveness.

- **Preventing Retaliatory Tariffs or [Quotas](../q/quota.md):** By agreeing to a VER, the exporting country can avoid more damaging [trade](../t/trade.md) restrictions from the importing country, such as harsh tariffs or [quotas](../q/quota.md). This can be beneficial for maintaining a more cooperative economic relationship between the two countries.

- **Stabilizing Markets:** By regulating the flow of goods, VERs can help stabilize prices and prevent [market](../m/market.md) disruption. This is particularly important in industries that are susceptible to fluctuations in [supply](../s/supply.md) and [demand](../d/demand.md).

- **Diplomatic Solutions:** VERs are often seen as diplomatic solutions to potential [trade](../t/trade.md) conflicts. Instead of imposing unilaterally punitive measures, countries negotiate VERs to reach a mutually beneficial agreement.

The process usually involves the following steps:

1. **[Negotiation](../n/negotiation.md):** Both the exporting and importing countries enter into negotiations to discuss the terms and conditions of the VER. This includes the specific product categories, the [duration](../d/duration.md) of the restraint, and the [volume](../v/volume.md) limits.

2. **Implementation:** Once agreed upon, the exporting country implements measures to restrict the quantity of goods exported. This could be done through various means such as licensing or [export](../e/export.md) certificates.

3. **Monitoring and Enforcement:** Both parties monitor the adherence to the agreement. Any breaches can lead to further negotiations or the imposition of more stringent [trade](../t/trade.md) barriers by the importing country.

## Advantages and Disadvantages

### Advantages

1. **Flexible and Adjustable:** VERs can be adjusted according to the changing economic environment, making them more flexible compared to rigid tariffs and [quotas](../q/quota.md).

2. **Creates [Goodwill](../g/goodwill.md):** By resolving [trade](../t/trade.md) issues through [negotiation](../n/negotiation.md), VERs can foster international [goodwill](../g/goodwill.md) and cooperation, reducing the likelihood of [trade](../t/trade.md) wars.

3. **Temporary Measure:** VERs are typically set for a fixed period, providing a temporary measure that can be lifted once the domestic [industry](../i/industry.md) adapts.

4. **[Market](../m/market.md) Stabilization:** VERs can help stabilize [market](../m/market.md) prices by controlling the influx of foreign goods.

### Disadvantages

1. **Can Create [Market](../m/market.md) Inefficiencies:** By limiting competition, VERs can lead to higher prices for consumers and inefficiencies in the [market](../m/market.md).

2. **May Lead to [Trade](../t/trade.md) Diversion:** Exporters might divert their goods to other less-regulated markets, creating [trade](../t/trade.md) distortions and inefficiencies in global [trade](../t/trade.md) patterns.

3. **Can Be Politically Sensitive:** The implementation of a VER can be politically sensitive both domestically and internationally, and may not always be welcomed by all stakeholders.

4. **Potential for Corruption:** The process of issuing [export](../e/export.md) licenses and certificates can be prone to corruption and misuse.

## Economic Impacts

### On the Exporting Country

For the exporting country, a VER can have mixed impacts. On one hand, it averts harsher [trade](../t/trade.md) measures that could have a more severe economic impact. On the other hand, it limits the country's ability to exploit its [comparative advantage](../c/comparative_advantage_in_trading.md) fully and may restrict its [market](../m/market.md) growth. Additionally, the exporting country's industries might need to find alternative markets to replace the restricted [market](../m/market.md), which could involve additional costs and logistical challenges.

### On the Importing Country

The importing country benefits from the protection of its domestic industries, allowing them time to adapt and remain competitive. However, consumers in the importing country may face higher prices and less choice as a result of reduced [supply](../s/supply.md). Furthermore, domestic industries may become less efficient over time if they rely too heavily on protectionist measures instead of innovation and competitiveness.

## International Trade Relations

VERs have significant implications for international [trade](../t/trade.md) relations. They serve as a tool for managing [bilateral trade](../b/bilateral_trade.md) tensions and preventing the escalation of [trade](../t/trade.md) disputes. However, they can also be a source of tension if one party feels that the agreement is not being honored or if the terms are deemed unfair.

### Legal and Regulatory Considerations

According to the World [Trade](../t/trade.md) Organization (WTO) regulations, VERs are generally discouraged as they distort [trade](../t/trade.md) flows. The WTO prefers that [trade](../t/trade.md) issues be resolved through multilateral agreements rather than through restrictive bilateral deals. Article XI of the General Agreement on Tariffs and [Trade](../t/trade.md) (GATT), which is part of the WTO framework, prohibits quantitative restrictions. However, VERs, being voluntary, often exist in a grey area of international [trade](../t/trade.md) law.

## Modern Use and Trends

While the use of traditional VERs has decreased due to stricter regulations and the influence of the WTO, modern variants do exist. Countries might still reach similar agreements under different names or frameworks, particularly in sectors experiencing rapid changes or pressure from global competition.

For instance, the U.S. and Mexico have periodically renegotiated agreements related to agricultural [trade](../t/trade.md) that resemble VERs in their operation, intending to manage [supply](../s/supply.md) and price stability. Similarly, concerns related to unfair [trade](../t/trade.md) practices or [dumping](../d/dumping.md) may lead countries to negotiate similar voluntary restrictions.

## Conclusion

Voluntary [Export](../e/export.md) Restraints represent a complex intersection of [trade](../t/trade.md) policy, international relations, and economic strategy. They allow countries to manage sensitive [trade](../t/trade.md) issues without resorting to more aggressive measures like tariffs or [quotas](../q/quota.md), fostering a more diplomatic approach to resolving potential conflicts. However, they also introduce [market](../m/market.md) inefficiencies and can be politically sensitive, requiring careful [negotiation](../n/negotiation.md) and implementation. As the global [trade](../t/trade.md) environment continues to evolve, the relevance and application of VERs may also change, necessitating ongoing analysis and adaptation by both policymakers and [industry](../i/industry.md) stakeholders.
