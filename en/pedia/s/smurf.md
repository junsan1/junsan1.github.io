# Smurfing in Financial Trading and Money Laundering

Smurfing in the context of financial trading and [money laundering](../m/money_laundering.md) is a sophisticated method used to structure financial transactions in a way that they appear below the threshold levels that would trigger regulatory reporting or scrutiny. This technique is often employed to avoid detection by financial authorities and to launder [money](../m/money.md) illegally gained from sources such as drug trafficking, [tax evasion](../t/tax_evasion.md), or other illicit activities. The term "smurfing" originated from the idea that a large sum of [money](../m/money.md) is divided or structured into smaller, seemingly insignificant amounts just like the tiny blue characters from the cartoon, "The Smurfs."

## Definition and Mechanism

Smurfing, also known as structuring, involves breaking down a substantial amount of cash into smaller deposits and then making these deposits into one or [multiple](../m/multiple.md) financial institutions over a certain period in an attempt to avoid detection. In the context of [money laundering](../m/money_laundering.md), these smaller deposits are typically below the threshold of reporting requirements imposed by financial regulations, such as those specified in the United States by the [Bank](../b/bank.md) Secrecy Act (BSA). For instance, in the U.S., any cash [transaction](../t/transaction.md) over $10,000 must be reported to the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN).

## Steps Involved in Smurfing

1. **Initial Collection**: Accumulating a large sum of illicit [money](../m/money.md) from various illegal activities.
2. **Structuring the Deposits**: Dividing the large sum into smaller amounts, each under the mandatory reporting threshold.
3. **[Distribution](../d/distribution.md)**: These smaller amounts are then deposited into [bank](../b/bank.md) accounts over a planned period of time in a manner that avoids detection.
4. **Layering and Integration**: The funds are further transferred between different accounts or invested into legal financial instruments to obscure their origins.

## Regulatory Environment

Regulatory bodies across the globe have strict mandates to monitor and identify suspicious activities. Various laws and regulations have been put in place to counteract smurfing. Key among these are:

- **[Bank](../b/bank.md) Secrecy Act (BSA)**: Enacted in 1970 in the United States, this law requires financial institutions to assist U.S. government agencies in detecting and preventing [money laundering](../m/money_laundering.md). It mandates that these institutions report any cash transactions over $10,000.
- **Anti-[Money Laundering](../m/money_laundering.md) (AML)** Regulations**: Various AML regulations across different countries necessitate that financial institutions report any suspicious activities, regardless of the amount. [Enhanced due diligence](../e/enhanced_due_diligence.md) (EDD) is also employed to monitor high-[risk](../r/risk.md) accounts.
- **Financial Action Task Force (FATF)**: An international body that sets standards and promotes effective implementation of legal, regulatory, and operational measures for combating [money laundering](../m/money_laundering.md), terrorist [financing](../f/financing.md), and other related threats.

## Techniques Used in Smurfing

1. **Breaking Down Deposits**: Structurers often split large sums into deposits under $10,000 and place them in [multiple](../m/multiple.md) [bank](../b/bank.md) accounts.
2. **Use of [Multiple](../m/multiple.md) [Bank](../b/bank.md) Accounts**: Opening several [bank](../b/bank.md) accounts across different financial institutions increases the complexity of tracking funds.
3. **Depositing in Different Locations**: Making these smaller deposits in different cities or states to further complicate the tracking process.
4. **Involving [Multiple](../m/multiple.md) Persons (Smurfs)**: Employing several individuals to conduct these transactions to make it less noticeable.

## Impact and Risks

- **Financial Institutions**: Smurfing exposes financial institutions to significant risks, including potential legal and regulatory penalties if they [fail](../f/fail.md) to detect and report suspicious activities.
- **Economic Stability**: [Money laundering](../m/money_laundering.md) and related practices like smurfing can destabilize economies by diverting funds from legitimate economic activities to illicit purposes.
- **Social Costs**: Smurfing facilitates the continuation of criminal enterprises by making it easier for criminals to launder their ill-gotten gains, potentially funding further illegal activities.

## Preventive Measures

1. **Know Your [Customer](../c/customer.md) (KYC)**: Financial institutions must conduct thorough [due diligence](../d/due_diligence.md) to verify the identity of their clients and assess the risks associated with their financial transactions.
2. **AML Software**: Integrating sophisticated software that utilizes [machine learning algorithms](../m/machine_learning_algorithms_in_trading.md) to detect patterns consistent with smurfing and other types of structuring.
3. **Training and Awareness**: Regular training programs for employees to recognize and report suspicious activities.
4. **Collaboration with Authorities**: Working closely with regulatory bodies to ensure compliance and to report any detected financial irregularities.

## Real-life Examples and Case Studies

1. **HSBC and [Money Laundering](../m/money_laundering.md) Scandal**: HSBC was implicated in a [money laundering](../m/money_laundering.md) scandal in 2012 where they were found to have knowingly allowed over $670 billion in [wire transfers](../w/wire_transfers.md) and more than $9.4 billion in purchases of U.S. [currency](../c/currency.md) involving [money laundering](../m/money_laundering.md) activities. Despite their [internal controls](../i/internal_controls.md), smurfing practices were able to circumvent their monitoring systems.
2. **Liberty Reserve**: An extensive example of smurfing was observed in the Liberty Reserve case, where the [digital currency](../d/digital_currency.md) operator was shut down in 2013. Liberty Reserve was accused of laundering $6 billion through structuring transactions to stay below the radar of financial authorities.
