# Liquidity Ratio

[Liquidity ratios](../l/liquidity_ratios.md) are financial metrics used to evaluate a company's ability to pay off its short-term debts as they come due using its most [liquid](../l/liquid.md) assets. These ratios are crucial for both the company management and investors as they provide insights into the [financial health](../f/financial_health.md) of the company. The most common [liquidity ratios](../l/liquidity_ratios.md) include the [Current Ratio](../c/current_ratio.md), the [Quick Ratio](../q/quick_ratio.md), and the [Cash Ratio](../c/cash_ratio.md).

## Current Ratio

The [Current Ratio](../c/current_ratio.md), also known as the working [capital](../c/capital.md) ratio, measures the ability of a company to cover its short-term [obligations](../o/obligation.md) with its short-term assets. It is calculated using the formula:

```text
[Current Ratio](../c/current_ratio.md) = [Current Assets](../c/current_assets.md) / [Current Liabilities](../c/current_liabilities.md)
```

### Importance

A higher [current ratio](../c/current_ratio.md) indicates that the company has a sufficient buffer of assets to cover its short-term liabilities, suggesting good [liquidity](../l/liquidity.md) health. A ratio below 1 suggests that the company may struggle to settle its short-term commitments.

### Example

Suppose a company has [current assets](../c/current_assets.md) worth $200,000 and [current liabilities](../c/current_liabilities.md) worth $100,000. The [current ratio](../c/current_ratio.md) would be:

```text
[Current Ratio](../c/current_ratio.md) = $200,000 / $100,000 = 2
```

This ratio suggests the company has twice the resources needed to meet its [current liabilities](../c/current_liabilities.md).

## Quick Ratio

The [Quick Ratio](../q/quick_ratio.md), also known as the [acid-test ratio](../a/acid-test_ratio.md), is a more stringent measure of [liquidity](../l/liquidity.md) compared to the [current ratio](../c/current_ratio.md). It excludes [inventory](../i/inventory.md) from [current assets](../c/current_assets.md) since [inventory](../i/inventory.md) is not easily convertible to cash. The formula for the [quick ratio](../q/quick_ratio.md) is:

```text
[Quick Ratio](../q/quick_ratio.md) = ([Current Assets](../c/current_assets.md) - [Inventory](../i/inventory.md)) / [Current Liabilities](../c/current_liabilities.md)
```

### Importance

The [Quick Ratio](../q/quick_ratio.md) is beneficial for assessing a company's immediate [liquidity](../l/liquidity.md) position without considering [inventory](../i/inventory.md), which can be subject to various [market](../m/market.md) conditions affecting its convertibility to cash. A ratio above 1 implies that the company can meet its short-term [obligations](../o/obligation.md) without selling [inventory](../i/inventory.md).

### Example

If a company has [current assets](../c/current_assets.md) of $200,000, [inventory](../i/inventory.md) of $50,000, and [current liabilities](../c/current_liabilities.md) of $100,000, the [quick ratio](../q/quick_ratio.md) would be:

```text
[Quick Ratio](../q/quick_ratio.md) = ($200,000 - $50,000) / $100,000 = 1.5
```

This indicates the company can cover its [current liabilities](../c/current_liabilities.md) 1.5 times without relying on the [sale](../s/sale.md) of its [inventory](../i/inventory.md).

## Cash Ratio

The [Cash Ratio](../c/cash_ratio.md) is the most stringent [liquidity](../l/liquidity.md) ratio, considering only cash and [cash equivalents](../c/cash_equivalents.md). It is calculated as follows:

```text
[Cash Ratio](../c/cash_ratio.md) = Cash and [Cash Equivalents](../c/cash_equivalents.md) / [Current Liabilities](../c/current_liabilities.md)
```

### Importance

The [Cash Ratio](../c/cash_ratio.md) is a conservative metric that provides insights into a company's [solvency](../s/solvency.md) status, emphasizing its capacity to pay short-term [obligations](../o/obligation.md) using only the most [liquid](../l/liquid.md) assets. A higher [cash ratio](../c/cash_ratio.md) is favorable but having too high a ratio might indicate underutilization of resources.

### Example

Assume a company has cash and [cash equivalents](../c/cash_equivalents.md) of $50,000 and [current liabilities](../c/current_liabilities.md) of $100,000. The [cash ratio](../c/cash_ratio.md) is:

```text
[Cash Ratio](../c/cash_ratio.md) = $50,000 / $100,000 = 0.5
```

This means the company can cover only half of its [current liabilities](../c/current_liabilities.md) using cash and [cash equivalents](../c/cash_equivalents.md) alone.

## Practical Applications

### Business Management

For internal management, [liquidity ratios](../l/liquidity_ratios.md) are vital in ensuring the company maintains enough short-term assets to [handle](../h/handle.md) [obligations](../o/obligation.md), avoiding potential financial difficulties. Regular analysis helps in decision-making about [asset management](../a/asset_management.md), [dividend](../d/dividend.md) policies, and funding strategies.

### Investors

Investors and creditors use [liquidity ratios](../l/liquidity_ratios.md) to gauge the [risk](../r/risk.md) associated with [investing](../i/investing.md) or lending [money](../m/money.md) to the company. These ratios indicate whether the company can sustain operations and continue growing without facing [liquidity](../l/liquidity.md) crises.

### Regulatory Compliance

Certain industries and regulatory bodies require companies to maintain a minimum level of [liquidity](../l/liquidity.md) to safeguard against financial [insolvency](../i/insolvency.md). For example, banks are mandated to maintain [liquidity](../l/liquidity.md) coverage ratios to manage [risk](../r/risk.md).

## Limitations

While [liquidity ratios](../l/liquidity_ratios.md) are indispensable tools in [financial analysis](../f/financial_analysis.md), they have certain limitations:

1. **Static Figures**: [Liquidity ratios](../l/liquidity_ratios.md) typically use [balance sheet](../b/balance_sheet.md) data, reflecting only a specific point in time. They may not provide a complete picture of a companyâ€™s ongoing [liquidity](../l/liquidity.md) situation.
2. **[Industry](../i/industry.md) [Variability](../v/variability.md)**: Optimal [liquidity ratios](../l/liquidity_ratios.md) vary significantly across industries. For example, a high [current ratio](../c/current_ratio.md) might be expected in [utility](../u/utility.md) companies but could indicate inefficiency in [manufacturing](../m/manufacturing.md) firms.
3. **Excluding Future Cash Flows**: These ratios do not account for future cash inflows and outflows, potentially [offering](../o/offering.md) a skewed view of [liquidity](../l/liquidity.md).
